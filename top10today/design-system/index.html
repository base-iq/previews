01 — The mark

Logo system: the spotlight ten

One pictorial device: a numeral 10 in Abril Fatface inside a ring — the spotlight, the medal, the cue card. The wordmark letters are Public Sans 800, letter-spaced wide like a marquee. The masthead ring always holds “10”; item rings hold their rank.

Stacked lockup — primary (mastheads, site header, newsletter header)

Variants: on dark — white wordmark, Marquee Mint ring and numeral. On light — Midnight Teal wordmark, Signal Teal ring and numeral. Mint never appears on light backgrounds (1.5:1 — decorative-invisible); teal never appears on the midnight ground (too close in luminance — the ring goes muddy).

Horizontal lockup — tight spaces (nav bars, doc headers)

Ring icon — avatars, favicons, compact placements

Default: midnight field + mint ring (works on light and dark surroundings). Tint field + teal ring when the dark disc clashes. The icon is always the double circle — disc + ring — so it survives at 32px.

Usage rules

  • Clear space: half the ring’s diameter on all sides.
  • Minimum sizes: stacked lockup 96px tall; horizontal 140px wide; icon 32px.
  • The masthead ring always holds 10. Rank numerals in rings belong to list items, never to the logo.
  • Don’t set the numeral in Public Sans, square the ring, fill the ring on light backgrounds, add glow or shadow to the mark, rotate, or recolor outside the two variants.
  • Don’t use the icon where space allows a full lockup.
  • Files (live, produced 2026-07-16): top10today-stacked-dark/-light, top10today-horiz-dark/-light, top10today-icon-dark/-light — SVG (vector paths, no font dependency) + PNG 2x each, at previews/top10today/assets/logos/.
02 — Color

Palette: house lights and one spotlight

Light pages, dark frame. Midnight Teal is the house lights — masthead, finale ring, footer — never a body-page background. Signal Teal is the working accent on light; Marquee Mint is the accent on dark, and only on dark. The governing discipline: the dark band frames, the spotlight points, everything between is calm.

Why success shares the accent: the brand’s teal is already an affirmative green — a second green would compete with it. Errors get the one warm-adjacent color in the system, and it appears nowhere else (it’s the gong — the act is over). Distinct from AmericanAdvisor’s crimson #8A2331 and LocalUpdate’s signal red #A8231C: brighter, browner, and never decorative.

Territory check: teal + mint sit squarely in the reserved cool/playful band — no warm hues (BaseLocal, AmericanAdvisor, TodayLocal), nowhere near Wire Cobalt #2036B1. White on Signal Teal: 6.1:1 — reversed button type is always safe.

03 — Type

Typography: the didone is rationed

Two faces. Abril Fatface is the theater — numerals, the finale headline, the masthead ten. Public Sans is everything else: headlines, body, UI — plain, sturdy, government-form honest. The rule that keeps the system credible: Abril never sets a full sentence except the finale headline. If the didone spreads, the brand tips into perfume-ad.

Heading style: sentence case everywhere; all-caps lives only in the small furniture (stamps, meta lines, the wordmark). Headlines never exclaim — the countdown position is the volume. Numerals in running text are lining figures from Public Sans; ranked numerals are always Abril.

Email typography — designed on the fallback

Gmail and Outlook don’t render web fonts; only Apple Mail does. The email stack is therefore designed on faces every device ships: Georgia inherits the didone role (a newspaper serif with real contrast — the theatrical register survives), Helvetica/Arial inherits Public Sans. Web faces listed first as progressive enhancement.

Ring numerals in email: CSS circles die in Outlook (border-radius unsupported — rings become squares). Solution: the rank badges ship as a pre-rendered PNG set — ten outline rings (1–10) in each color variant plus the filled finale ring — produced once at delivery and reused in every edition forever, since the numbers never change. Alt text is the numeral (alt="7"); images-off readers get a live-text “7.” in Georgia before the headline. Wordmark is always a PNG in email.

05 — The unit

The ranked item: one unit, three sizes

The most-repeated component in the brand — number + headline + blurb. Every ranked list counts down to #1, at every size. A three-item section runs 3-2-1; the flat evening ten runs 10→1. The drumroll is the constraint that makes the brand: the best thing is always the finale, and the finale ring marks it.

Rules of the countdown

  • Every list descends. Rank 1 is always the last item rendered. No exceptions — ascending order breaks the brand’s one promise.
  • One finale ring per email or page, on the edition’s single most important #1. In a multi-section morning edition, the lead section’s #1 takes it; other sections end with a standard outline ring at #1.
  • Blurbs are 1–3 sentences. If it needs more, it’s an article, not a ranked item — link out.
  • Placements are ranked items. A paid item occupies a rank position — it’s #6, or #4, whatever the editor assigns. There is no “between ranks.” The countdown is the countdown.
06 — Motion

Motion: the drumroll, once

One move in the whole system: when the finale enters the viewport, its ring pulses a single soft mint wave — 900ms, ease-out, once. Everything else is color-only at 120ms (hovers, links). Under prefers-reduced-motion, even the pulse is cut. The page header and the finale demo above run the pulse live — replay it here.

1

The pulse — 900ms, once

Why so little: the audience is 50+, the promise is “worth your time,” and a theater only dims the lights once a night. The single pulse gives the drumroll a physical beat without turning the email into a slideshow (in email, where animation is unreliable, the finale’s staging — stamp, filled ring, Abril headline — carries the beat alone).
