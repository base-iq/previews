2

How brokers turn it into a product

On their own, those scraps are harmless. Stitched together, they become a profile detailed enough to impersonate you.

A finished profile can include:

  • Your name, age, and current address
  • Past addresses and phone numbers
  • Relatives and known associates
  • What you buy, own, and search for

By recent estimates the trade moves about $290 billion a year worldwide, run by more than 4,000 data brokers. Most of it is legal, and most of them will never tell you they hold a file on you.

The file never goes stale, either. Every new breach refills it.

Every record year hands the brokers more to sell. Incogni re-files your removals every 60 to 90 days, so the listings that creep back keep getting pulled down.

3

Who's buying

Once a profile exists, it sells to almost anyone.

  • Advertisers, to aim products at you
  • People-search sites, to publish your profile for anyone to look up
  • Background-check and risk firms, to vet you for businesses
  • Scammers, to make a fraud sound like it already knows you
4

What it costs you

This is where the fraud comes in. The richer your profile, the more dangerous and convincing scammers can be. Some common tactics include:

  • Imposter calls that already know your name, address, and relatives, so a fake family emergency sounds real.
  • Government impersonation, posing as Social Security, Medicare, or the IRS with just enough detail to rattle you.
  • Account takeover, using your personal facts to answer security questions and get into your accounts.
  • Phishing texts and emails that name a real purchase, bank, or street you would recognize.
  • New accounts in your name, from credit cards to loans you never applied for.

Each one works because it sounds like it already knows you. Older adults are targeted most, and lose the most.

About $38,000 was the average loss reported by fraud victims over 60 in 2025, and 12,400 of them lost more than $100,000 each.

A few honest questions

What is a data broker, exactly?

A company that gathers personal information about you from public records, purchases, apps, and other businesses, bundles it into a profile, and sells it. A few you've heard of. Most you haven't, and that's by design.

How did they get my information?

Rarely from you directly. It comes from property and voter records, court filings, store loyalty programs, app permissions you tapped past, and other companies that resold what they had. You almost never agreed to the part where it gets packaged and sold.

Is any of this legal?

Mostly, yes. Buying and selling personal data is legal across most of the U.S. A growing set of state laws gives you the right to opt out, but the burden sits on you to ask, broker by broker, and enforcement is thin.

Will removing my data stop scams?

It lowers your odds. It doesn't drop them to zero. Fewer listings mean fewer ways for a stranger to find you and sound like they know you. A determined fraud or a fresh breach can still reach you, so treat removal as one strong layer, not a force field.
