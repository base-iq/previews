The canonical presentation — the sun-up masthead: horizon rule, marigold half-disc rising through it, wordmark, dateline. This stack opens the site and every edition. The sun sits on the news, not instead of it.

01 · Wordmark

One word, one sun

“TodayLocal” set in Fraunces 700, tracked slightly tight (−0.015em), always one color. The warmth comes from the letterforms — Fraunces’s soft terminals — and from the sun device above; the wordmark itself never goes marigold (a yellow wordmark at text sizes would fail contrast everywhere it matters).

TodayLocal
Cream variant — dark backgrounds (Roast, photography). The sun stays marigold: 6.7:1 on Roast.

Usage: minimum clear space = the cap height of the “T” on all sides. Full wordmark never below 140px wide on screen. The horizon + sun may be dropped in cramped placements (nav bars), leaving the bare wordmark — but the canonical stack is the default. Never restyle in another face, never letter-space wide, never recolor outside the two variants.

03 · Color

Warm ink on dawn cream; marigold rationed like sunlight

The governing discipline: most of any surface is Roast on Dawn Cream. Marigold appears where the sun would — the masthead, the TODAY flag, the end mark — and almost nowhere else. Deep Gold does the everyday clickable work. When two things would both be marigold, the less important one goes Deep Gold or Roast.

04 · Typography

Fraunces speaks, Karla reads

Fraunces — a display serif with soft, warm terminals — is the features-desk voice: headlines, the wordmark, pull moments. Karla — a humanist grotesque with a hand-drawn undertone — does all the reading: body, UI, captions. Two families, no third. Neither appears anywhere else in the portfolio.

Heading rules

Sentence case everywhere; all-caps lives only in the small furniture (kickers, datelines, the TODAY flag). Headlines never exclaim — morning energy comes from present-tense verbs, not punctuation. Display sizes track slightly tight; body never tracks.

Email typography — designed on the fallback

Gmail and Outlook don’t render web fonts; only Apple Mail does. So the email stack is designed to degrade in one deliberate move: Fraunces → Georgia (both warm, high-contrast serifs — the register survives) and Karla → Helvetica/Arial. The wordmark is always a PNG in email. Side-by-side proof:

05 · Morning furniture

The devices that make it TodayLocal

The horizon rule

A 1.5–2px Roast rule opens every major surface — the masthead carries the sun; section breaks repeat the bare rule. Wheat 1px rules handle quiet separation (list items, cards). Depth comes from rules and tint panels, never shadows.

The TODAY flag

The brand promise as a device: a small marigold chip — Today — marking items that happen or expire today. Strictly rationed: if everything is today, nothing is. Never more than a third of the items on any surface.

The daybreak band

Monetized and service sections sit on a full-bleed Honey Tint panel — different stock for different content, marked honestly:

The sundown mark

Every edition closes with the sun going down through the rule — the masthead device flipped. The edition is over; tomorrow’s sun comes up in the next one. A small structural joke nobody else in the portfolio can make:

Buttons & interaction

Deep Gold primary (one per viewport), Roast-frame secondary. Radius is a soft 4px — friendlier than the siblings’ hard corners, never a pill. Focus ring: 2px Deep Gold outline, 2px offset, everywhere.

Get today’s edition  See yesterday’s

Motion — the morning stretch

Content fades up gently on entry (240ms, ease-out, 8px rise, once) — the page wakes up rather than snapping on. Hovers change color only, 150ms. Nothing bounces, nothing lingers: upbeat is quick, not busy. prefers-reduced-motion collapses everything to instant.

Photography

Morning light, literally — golden-hour warmth, real places open for the day: the market setting up, the diner counter at 7am, the trailhead before the heat. Documentary, never lifestyle-stock. People 50+ appear as the capable subjects of the day. Every editorial photo takes a Field Gray caption.
